As a final note, I'd like to push an alternative form to the power rule.\
The derivative of x to the power of n over n factorial is x to the power of n minus one over n minus one factorial.

This form takes 1 to zero.\
x to 1.\
x to the power of two over two to x.\
And so on.\

This forms a chain of relations.\ 
These powers are foundational to higher order mathematics. They appear alot.\
For example they are important to the taylor series.

If you take these powers and pair them to the corresponding derivative summing them together that is the taylor series.\
An example of this pairing takes on the form of\
the sum of h to the power of "a" over "a" factorial times the "b"th derivative of f of x. Where "a" is equal to "b" and are integers.

I am showing this variant of the taylor series because if you different it a very important symmetry is represented.\
That h and x are algebraically interchangeable in the formulation.\
Some may find this obvious given that I present the series as equal to f of x plus h. And x and h commute and thus are literally interchangeable.\
However, I haven't actually proved this fact yet.\ 
And I think it's important to understand this from the perspective of the taylor series.\

So:\
Differentiating by x in this form increases "b" by one.\
Differentiating by h in this form decreases "a" by one.\
Since both "a" and "b" were increments of the integers, these two transformations are actually equal.
In both "b" ends up as one more than "a".
